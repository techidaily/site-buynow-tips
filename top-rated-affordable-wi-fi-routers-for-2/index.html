What to Look For in a Budget Wi-Fi Router in 2024

The name of the game when it comes to picking a budget wireless router is balancing cost against features. While there’s certainly a lot of overlap with non-budget routers , having a good understanding of what features are necessary and what features are “nice to haves” will ultimately dictate how much you will save.

For example, if you plan to connect many devices to your router, then MU-MIMO is a core feature you’ll want to have. Another is Quality of Service (QoS) , which allows you to set bandwidth priorities for different devices and is incredibly useful in a busy household.

Alternatively, if you have a single gaming PC or 4K TV, Wi-Fi 6 and beamforming technology can help achieve speeds and stability that serve both those needs.

One thing we will suggest is to try and push your budget as much as you can. It will make a difference if you can get a slightly better router or speeds by spending an extra $10 or $15. Going from 600Mbps to 700Mbps might not seem like much, but that extra 100Mbps might give you a bit of a longer range to work with.

Best Budget Router Overall: TP-Link Archer AX3000 (AX55)

TP-Link

An upgrade over the AX50 , the TP-Link Archer AX3000 (AX55) offers OneMesh support and better stability over an already great budget router.

First, in terms of real-world speed, 5GHz can hit 500Mbps at around 40 feet, whereas 2.4GHz is likely to only get you around 100Mbps at the same distance. Interestingly, though, the AX55 swaps out the internal Intel chip for a Qualcomm one, which, while operating within a smaller frequency, manages to be much more stable as a result.

As for features, you’ll find everything in the previous iteration and then some. The most important new addition is the support for WPA3 , which is excellent for security. You also get OneMesh support, which means that you can pair it with routers and Wi-Fi extenders that support TP-Link’s standard.

Other additions are denial-of-service (DoS) protection through Homeshield as well as DNS relay. Unfortunately, you do lose the external memory slot that the AX50 came with, but it’s definitely worth what you get in return.

As for general connectivity, you have the same four-gigabit ethernet ports in the back, four nice big antennas with beamforming, and a USB 3.0 port. The AX55 also has support for Wi-Fi 6 , which lets it reach such high speeds, even at a range of 40 feet. Finally, it also has support for MU-MIMO, which means you can connect to several devices simultaneously without experiencing weird latency or lag.

The TP-Link Archer AX21 is a great example of why you don’t need to spend big bucks on getting a solid Wi-Fi router. Featuring Wi-Fi 6 support, this dual-band router delivers fast data speeds and has a strong signal transmission range.

The Archer AX21 is also easy to install and supports the company’s OneMesh technology, which allows it to make a mesh network with compatible routers, range extenders , and powerline adapters . There are over 50 OneMesh-compatible devices in TP-Link’s portfolio.

It has an elegant all-black design, four non-removable antennas, and a decent selection of ports, including four Gigabit LAN ports, one Gigabit WAN port, and a USB 2.0 Type-A port. Wall mount support is also available.

In other features, this budget TP-Link offering can hit a theoretical maximum speed of 574Mbps on the 2.4GHz band and 1,201Mbps on the 5GHz band. But in the real world, you will get considerably lower speeds, making it most suitable for around 500Mbps internet connection. Our best overall pick will better serve you for faster broadband connections.

TP-Link also had to do some cost-cutting to make the AX21 this affordable. So, it doesn’t support 160MHz channel width or has the company’s HomeShield security service. But you still get basic parental controls.

Best Budget Gaming Router: ASUS RT-AX1800S

ASUS

Pros Cons
Excellent performance at 2.4 and 5GHz No WPA3 security standard
Support for MU-MIMO and OFDMA
Can be converted into a mesh node

The levels of speed and performance you can get from a router that costs under $100 are quite incredible these days. If you had searched a “Wi-Fi 6 gaming router” just 12 months ago, the average price would be double or even triple the cost of the impressive ASUS RT-AX1800S .

It might not be the fastest Wi-Fi 6 router in terms of raw speed, but with 2.4 and 5GHz speeds of up to 574Mbps and 1201Mbps, it’s certainly no slouch. Of course, maximum speeds and achievable speeds are two different things. Luckily, the RT-AX1800S has a few tricks to help close the gap between the two.

Firstly, there’s MU-MIMO , which allows the router to communicate with multiple devices simultaneously, reducing bandwidth bottlenecks. It also supports OFDMA and 1024-QAM, Wi-Fi 6 technologies that intelligently allocate bandwidth based on the application. That makes the network more stable and less prone to fluctuation, which is essential for a good online gaming experience.

The router is relatively understated in design, although you still get four adjustable antennae to help direct signal where needed. On the back, you’ll find a gigabit WAN port and four gigabit LAN ports for any devices that require a wired connection. And with a quick firmware update, you can also easily link up two or more routers for wider network coverage using AiMesh.

The only real downside of the RT-AX1800S is that it doesn’t support the WPA3 security standard , a feature you often find on more expensive Wi-Fi 6 routers. That said, WPA2 is usually perfectly fine for domestic use, and this omission shouldn’t be a major cause for concern.

While it’s true that Wi-Fi 6 tends to come with a hefty price tag, it’s still possible to get a router that supports it for less than $100, which is where the TP-Link Archer AX1500 (AX10) comes in to save the day.

Of course, you probably won’t be surprised to hear that 2.4Ghz band performance isn’t great, likely netting you around 80Mbps at 30 feet. It shines the most in the 5GHz department, where it can easily hit around 750Mbps at 30 feet, most likely due to Wi-Fi 6. Stability is also pretty good for an $80 router, so there are no complaints. It even has WPA3 support, a feature it received in a firmware update .

As for setup, it’s relatively simple and follows the same system as all TP-Link Archer routers, so if you’ve used one of those before, you’ll be right at home here. There are many advanced controls, too, such as port forwarding, VPN support, and dynamic DNS . That being said, you can easily plug the router in and let it do its own thing if you don’t have the tech skills.

Other than that, the only real significant downsides are a lack of a USB port and support for 160Mhz frequency, which are not dealbreakers for most.

Best Budget Mesh Router: TP-Link Deco X20

TP-Link

Pros Cons
Small and easy to place around the house No dedicated backchannel
Great range Lack of USB ports
Relatively cheap Performance is only OK for price

Wi-Fi dead zones in the house can be incredibly frustrating, especially if you only need a few extra feet to connect in your bed or a specific sofa in the living room. While Wi-Fi range extenders are great, mesh systems like the TP-Link Deco X20 provide a seamless Wi-Fi experience in their own right.

Coming in at $177 for a set of two, the Deco X20 can cover 4,000 square feet of your home pretty handily. The 5GHz speeds of the base router unit will see you get up to 650Mbps, while the satellite throughput will give you up to 450Mbps at close range. These speeds could have been higher, but TP-Link sadly didn’t include a dedicated backchannel, and while it is a missed opportunity, speeds should be more than enough for the majority of users.

If the coverage of the two-pack doesn’t cover it, there is a three-pack on offer for $230, which should bump that up to 5800 Sq.Ft., an area that most homes will likely not have to worry about.

As for features, it’s pretty jam-packed, having QoS, MI-MIMO, WPA3 , and even Orthogonal Frequency-Division Multiple Access (OFDMA), which is just a fancy way of saying “multiple slow-speed connections.” The admin app is also great—TP-Link knows how to make easy-to-use software, and Homecare is no different.

If the price for the Deco X20 is too high, the TP-Link Deco Mesh Wi-Fi System (Deco M5) is a slightly cheaper mesh router option. You’re giving up Wi-Fi 6 and some performance, but if you don’t have several hundred Mbps speeds, this three-pack comes in at $140, which is even cheaper than the Deco X20 two-pack.

The 5GHz base station starts at 450Mbps at close range and starts decreasing to around 250Mbps at 30-feet, and as for the nodes, you’re looking at similar performance at 30-feet, although maybe closer to 225Mbps.

FAQ

What does a Wi-Fi router do?

A Wi-Fi router takes the wired broadband Internet connection coming into your home, often via a modem, and turns it into a wireless signal. This signal can then be connected to remotely by any device with a Wi-Fi antenna, such as phones, tablets, and computers.

How do I reset a Wi-Fi router?

Wi-Fi routers almost always have a reset button on the back, usually next to the power button and LAN ports. Some reset buttons can easily be pressed with your finger, but others are set within a small hole and require a pin or paperclip to reach them.

How does my modem affect my router?

In most home networks, the modem is the primary connection point between the incoming broadband connection and your Wi-Fi router. A modem not rated for the same connection speeds as your router can cause a bottleneck and limit the bandwidth available to your devices.

What is Wi-Fi 6?

Wi-Fi 6 is the latest version of the wireless internet protocol that was rolled out in 2019. Capable of transmitting data at a maximum of 9.6Gbps, it is a big improvement on Wi-Fi 5, which was capped at 3.5Gbps. Wi-Fi 6 is also more efficient than earlier versions and is better suited to simultaneously handling data to and from multiple devices.
